Time-Series Forecasting · Corporación Favorita (Ecuador)

Predicting supermarket sales — without fooling yourself

Real data is rarely "wrong" in a way that throws an error. It's wrong in ways that silently make your model worse and give you no clue why. This guide walks you from the simplest question — "what are we even predicting?" — up to the subtle traps that separate a good time-series model from a broken one.

1,782 series 16-day horizon 28,512 predictions scored by RMSLE

Every number in this guide is real — reproduced from the competition CSVs. A few prediction examples are labelled illustrative (we have no trained model yet); everything else is verified.

Lesson 1

What are we actually predicting?

A supermarket chain in Ecuador wants to predict daily sales. There are 54 stores, each selling 33 product families (BEVERAGES, PRODUCE, AUTOMOTIVE, …), recorded once per day.

Given years of history (2013-01-01 → 2017-08-15), predict sales for the next 16 days (2017-08-16 → 2017-08-31) for every store + family. That fixed window has a name we reuse everywhere: the horizon (= 16 days).

Key word · series

A series is the day-by-day sales history of one product family at one store — the "thing that has a past and a future." Almost every operation happens per series, never mixing across them.

54 stores × 33 families = 1,782 series
16 days × 1,782 series  = 28,512 predictions   ← exactly the rows we submit

Lesson 3 · the heart of it

The six silent traps

Ordered easiest → subtlest. Each follows the same shape: what you see → why it quietly hurts → the fix → where it lives in code. A theme runs through several — leakage: letting the model use information it would not have at prediction time.

▶1 Missing calendar days (the counting trap) ✅ Fixed

What you see: the store closes on some days — notably every Dec 25 — so those dates have no row at all. They're absent, not recorded as a zero. Series store=1, AUTOMOTIVE has 1,684 rows over a 1,688-day span → 4 missing days, all Christmases.

Why it quietly hurts: time-series features count rows. After a gap, "7 rows back" is no longer "7 days back" — your "same weekday last week" lag points at the wrong day, and seasonal sine/cosine terms slide out of phase. Nothing errors; the model just gets worse.

The fix: rebuild each series onto a complete daily calendar and fill the missing days with sales = 0 (the store sold nothing — which is true, and keeps the row spacing regular). Tag each inserted row was_closed = True so nothing is lost.

Read the numbers — they're the proof

original rows   : 3,000,888    ← what Kaggle gave us
reindexed rows  : 3,008,016    ← after filling gaps
expected        : 1,782 × 1,688 = 3,008,016
rows inserted   :     7,128    ← 4 per series (the four Christmases)
  3,000,888  (gaps where the store was shut)
+     7,128  (4 Christmases × 1,782 series, restored as sales = 0)
─────────
  3,008,016  (a complete, gap-free grid: 1,782 series × 1,688 days)

A complete grid must have exactly (series × days) rows. The total matching the product — and 7,128 dividing evenly by 1,782 — is itself the evidence the reindex did exactly the right thing.

Where: src/data.py :: reindex_series_gapfree(), checked by src/validation.py :: assert_gapfree().

▶2 Oil price gaps + blanks (your first taste of leakage) ⏳ Planned

What you see: Ecuador's economy is oil-dependent, so oil price is a plausible driver. But oil.csv has two holes: oil only trades on business days (weekends/holidays have no row), and 43 rows exist with an empty price.

The fix: build a continuous daily calendar, left-join oil, then forward-fill — carry the last known price forward (one leading back-fill for the very first blank day).

Leakage warning

Why not interpolation? It averages the value before and after the gap — and "after" is the future. Using it means the model peeks at information it wouldn't have. Forward-fill only looks backward, so it's safe.

Where: oil features in src/features.py.

▶3 Holidays aren't a simple yes/no (domain modeling) ⏳ Planned

What you see: holidays_events.csv looks like a "was it a holiday?" table, but isn't. A row with transferred = True means the holiday did not happen on its listed date — a separate Transfer row carries the real date. There are also Bridge days and Work Day rows, and locale (National/Regional/Local) means a holiday doesn't apply to every store.

The fix: compute an effective holiday calendar — drop the transferred originals, honor the Transfer/Bridge/Work Day rows, and scope by locale: National → all stores; Regional → that state; Local → that city.

Lesson

A column named like a feature isn't automatically a usable feature. Understanding the domain is part of feature engineering.

Where: holiday features in src/features.py.

▶4 The 2016 earthquake anomaly ⏳ Planned

What you see: a magnitude-7.8 earthquake on 2016-04-16 caused a relief-driven sales spike lasting weeks.

Why it quietly hurts: a one-off spike isn't a repeating pattern. Left unmarked, your trend and seasonality try to "explain" it and get bent out of shape.

The fix: add a binary (or decaying) earthquake-window flag (~2016-04-16 → mid-May) so the model absorbs the spike as a known event. We do not delete those rows — that would re-open a hole in the gap-free index from Trap 1.

Lesson

Don't delete anomalies — label them.

Where: calendar features in src/features.py.

▶5 New / sparse series (the coverage trap) ⏳ Planned

What you see: some store + family pairs have little or no meaningful history (a barely-selling family, or one that only started recently).

Why it quietly hurts: a complex model can't learn a pattern that isn't there — but the submission must contain a value for all 28,512 rows. A missing/NaN prediction is invalid.

The fix: for thin series, fall back to a simple seasonal-naive / near-zero prediction. See Baselines for exactly how these work.

Where: the fallback path in src/models.py.

▶6 Transactions are past-only (a hard constraint) ⚠️ Constraint

What you see: transactions.csv is rich and tempting — but it is not provided for the 16-day test horizon. You don't know a store's transaction count on a day you're predicting.

Leakage warning

Use same-day transactions and validation looks fantastic — because in validation you happen to have that column. On the real future it doesn't exist, so the model collapses.

The fix: use transactions only as a lagged feature (e.g. transactions 7+ days ago, which are known by prediction time).

Where: lag features in src/features.py.

Lesson 4

How do we know a model is good? An honest scoreboard

A model always looks brilliant on the data it trained on — it has effectively seen the answers. The only honest signal is its error on data it did not train on. We keep a private slice of our own data as an instant, unlimited scoreboard: a holdout.

Time-series rule

Random K-fold cheats here. Validate on a day in the middle of history and the model trains on days that come after it — it sees the future relative to what it predicts. The split must be a clean cut in time.

train: 2013-01-01 ........... 2017-07-30  │  holdout: 2017-07-31 → 2017-08-15
       (model learns from the past)        │  (model predicts forward, blind)

We hold out the last 16 training days — same length, same "predict 16 days forward" shape as the real task.

The holdout is NOT test.csv — keep them straight

Holdouttest.csv
Comes fromcarved out of train.csva separate file Kaggle provides
Dates2017-07-31 → 2017-08-152017-08-16 → 2017-08-31
Know the real sales?Yes — we just hide themNo — that's what we predict
Who scores itwe do, instantly, locallyKaggle, after we submit
Purposeprivate rehearsal to compare modelsthe actual graded exam
Then retrain on everything

The last 16 days are hidden only while comparing models. Once the best approach is picked, re-fit it on all training data (including those last 16 days) before predicting the real test.csv — the most recent days carry the freshest trend and seasonality.

Lesson 5

The five rules that keep us honest

Everything above distills into five rules — and they're enforced in code, so a mistake fails loudly instead of silently corrupting a score.

  1. No leakage. Every feature must be knowable at prediction time. Lags use strictly past days; oil is forward-filled, never interpolated; transactions are lag-only. Guard: assert_no_leak().
  2. Gap-free before features. The Trap-1 reindex runs before any lag or seasonal feature, so "7 rows back" always means "7 days back." Guard: assert_gapfree().
  3. Lags must clear the horizon (≥ 16 days). The last day we predict (Aug 31) is 16 days past our last real data (Aug 15). We forecast all 16 days at once (direct forecasting) with a base lag of 16. See the lag-horizon deep dive.
  4. Time-respecting validation. One 16-day holdout mirroring the real horizon; train strictly on earlier data; random K-fold forbidden.
  5. Consistent metric handling. RMSLE computed on log1p, predictions clipped ≥ 0, applied identically to every model so scores are truly comparable.
Meta-lesson

When a rule matters, encode it as an assertion. A guard that raises the moment you break a rule is worth more than a comment reminding you not to.

Deep dive · start here

Forecast baselines: seasonal-naive & near-zero

The simplest possible ways to predict — no training, no maths. We use them as fallback rules for series too thin to model (Trap 5). They predict the future 16 days; they do not change any past data.

how it works Seasonal-naive = copy last week forward

Naive = don't learn anything; copy the past forward. Seasonal = copy from the same point in the previous cycle (here, the weekly cycle).

predict next Monday  = last Monday's sales
predict next Tuesday = last Tuesday's sales
…repeat last week's 7-day pattern across the 16-day horizon

Sample data — store 1, GROCERY I (real)

The last known week (7 days ending Tue Aug 15, 2017):

Last weekWed 8/9Thu 8/10Fri 8/11Sat 8/12Sun 8/13Mon 8/14Tue 8/15
sales271925911270163095224072508

Seasonal-naive copies each weekday forward — the 7-day pattern just repeats across the horizon, preserving this series' own shape (here a Sunday dip to 952 against midweek highs of ~2.5–2.7k).

how it works Near-zero = "sells nothing → predict nothing"

If a series has essentially no sales ever, the safest forecast is ≈ 0. Example — store 1, BOOKS, whose last week is all zeros:

Last 7 days (store 1, BOOKS)0000000

→ predict 0 for all 16 horizon days. Nothing to copy.

important Who decides which rule? (not the model)

A common misconception: the trained model does not choose between the main prediction, seasonal-naive, and near-zero. The choice is made before modeling by a simple rule-based gate (an if check) on each series' history. Thin series are peeled off and never reach the model.

For each of the 1,782 series:
   ├─ Has enough history?  ──►  use the MAIN model (the learned one)
   └─ Too thin?            ──►  use a FALLBACK rule
                                   ├─ a little history to copy  ►  seasonal-naive
                                   └─ basically no sales ever   ►  near-zero (~0)

A trained model is a function: it always returns a number — it can't output a blank. The risk for a no-history series isn't emptiness; it's that the number is unreliable garbage. So we route thin series around the model (the cleaner option), train only on healthy ones, then stitch both prediction sets back together into all 28,512 rows.

Deep dive · easy–medium · interactive

The metric: RMSLE

Read the name backwards and it's a recipe, one word per step:

WordStep
Errorhow wrong each prediction is: prediction − actual
Logarithmicbut first take the log of both numbers
Squaredsquare each error (too-high and too-low both count)
Meanaverage all those squared errors
Roottake the square root at the end
 
RMSLE = sqrt( mean( (log1p(prediction) − log1p(actual))² ) )
Why the log?

It measures relative (ratio) error, not the raw gap. Predict 15 when truth is 5, and predict 3,000 when truth is 1,000 — both are "3× too high," equally bad. Because log(a) − log(b) = log(a/b), RMSLE asks "how many times off were you?" That's how a tiny family and a huge family count fairly.

log1p(x) = log(1+x) so log1p(0)=0 (no crash on zero sales); its inverse expm1 returns real units. Two consequences we apply to every model: we train in log space, and we clip predictions to ≥ 0.

Deep dive · medium

Seasonality & Fourier features

A seasonality is a repeating pattern tied to the calendar. This data has two: weekly (across all series, weekends sell more on average — per series-day means Sat ≈ 433, Sun ≈ 463 vs ≈ 285–346 on weekdays) and yearly (a December surge). Individual series still vary — store 1's GROCERY I actually dips on Sunday.

Weekly shape — store 1, GROCERY I (mean sales by weekday, real)

Roughly flat Mon–Sat (~2.3–2.8k) with a sharp Sunday dip to ~1031. A weekend-peaking series would simply have a different shape — and the model adapts per series.

how it works Why sin + cos represent a cycle

A cycle is a wave; the natural way to write one is sin and cos. For a weekly pattern (period = 7): sin(2π·day/7) and cos(2π·day/7) each complete one full wave every 7 days. Why both? A single sine has a fixed peak position; combining a sin and a cos (with weights the model learns) lets it place the peak anywhere, at any height — so with just 2 numbers per cycle a plain linear regression fits "this series peaks Saturday, that one Sunday."

Sample data — exact sin/cos vs real weekday means

The model learns two weights (w_sin, w_cos) plus an intercept so intercept + w_sin·sin + w_cos·cos traces this shape. Two columns reconstruct the weekly rhythm and extend cleanly into any future date. (A sharp single-day dip like Sunday's needs a couple more Fourier pairs to pin down exactly.)

tooling CalendarFourier & DeterministicProcess

CalendarFourier generates those sin/cos columns from the dates. Instead of 365 separate "is it Jan 1 / Jan 2 / …" columns for the yearly cycle, you ask for a few Fourier pairs and get a handful of smooth columns approximating the whole annual wave.

DeterministicProcess assembles the full time-feature table: a constant, a trend (1, 2, 3, … counting upward), and the seasonal Fourier terms. "Deterministic" is the key word — these depend only on the date, not on past sales, so they're computable for any future day. Perfect for forecasting.

why gaps wreck it "Out of phase" — in numbers

Phase = where you are in the cycle. The sin/cos value comes from the row's position, assuming consecutive days. Correct (no gap):

datereal weekdayposition dsin usedsales
Aug 12Sat5−0.9751630
Aug 13Sun6−0.782952

Now drop the Sat Aug 12 row (a closed day). Everything after shifts up one:

datereal weekdayposition d (miscounted)sin usedsales
Aug 13Sun5 (thinks it's Sat)−0.975952

Sunday's 952 sales are now paired with Saturday's sin value. Every weekday past the gap is mislabelled by one — silent corruption.

Punchline

Fill the calendar first (so position = real date again), then build Fourier seasonality.

Deep dive · advanced

The 16-day lag horizon (why lags must be ≥ 16)

A lag feature ("sales N days ago") is built from past sales. But our prediction window is 16 days long and we have no real sales inside it. That puts a hard floor on how short a lag we can use.

TRAIN (real sales known)            TEST / horizon (must PREDICT, no real sales)
... Jul 31 ... Aug 15  │  Aug 16  Aug 17  ...  Aug 30  Aug 31
            ↑ last real day        └──────── 16 days to predict ────────┘
the problem Why lag_7 breaks partway through

Check lag_7 for each test day — does "7 days before" land in the known zone (≤ Aug 15)?

Predict this day7 days beforeKnown?
Aug 16Aug 9✅ in train
Aug 22Aug 15✅ last real day
Aug 23Aug 16❌ a test day — no real sales
Aug 31Aug 24❌ test day

From Aug 23 onward, "7 days ago" points back into the test period itself.

the fix Why lag_16 works for the whole horizon
Predict this day16 days beforeKnown?
Aug 16Jul 31✅ in train
Aug 31Aug 15✅ last real day

Even the farthest day (Aug 31) reaches back exactly to Aug 15.

Sample data — tail of store 1, GROCERY I (real)

dateJul 30Jul 31…Aug 14Aug 15 (last real)
sales10862966…24072508
predict daylag_16 source → valuelag_17 source → value
Aug 16Jul 31 → 2966Jul 30 → 1086
Aug 31Aug 15 → 2508Aug 14 → 2407

Every value comes from real data.

The rule

The last day you predict is 16 days past your last real data. A lag that must work for the whole horizon has to reach back at least 16 days. In general: minimum usable lag = horizon length (H).

The design decision: direct forecasting with base lag = 16

ApproachHowTrade-off
Direct (chosen)Predict all 16 days at once, using only real lags ≥ 16 (lag_16, lag_17, … ; rolling windows anchored at shift(16))One feature matrix, no error compounding
Recursive (not chosen)Predict Aug 16, treat it as real, use as lag_1 for Aug 17, …Allows short lags, but errors snowball across 16 days
Direct per stepA separate model per horizon day (day-1 uses lag_1, day-16 uses lag_16)Freshest legal lag per day, but 16 models
Long horizons → lean on seasonality

A 1-year horizon would need lag_365 as its shortest lag — nearly useless. The longer the horizon, the less lags help and the more you lean on date-only features (trend + Fourier seasonality, holidays), computable for any future date. That's why the modelling is staged: a deterministic backbone first, lags added on top as a short-horizon booster — never a replacement.

Where: lag/rolling features in src/features.py; enforced by the base-lag ≥ 16 guard in src/validation.py :: assert_no_leak().
