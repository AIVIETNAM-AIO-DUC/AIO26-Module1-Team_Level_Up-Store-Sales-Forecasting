Time-Series Forecasting · Corporación Favorita (Ecuador)

Predicting supermarket sales — without fooling yourself

Real data is rarely "wrong" in a way that throws an error. It's wrong in ways that silently make your model worse and give you no clue why. This guide walks the learner journey staged by tasks.md: Foundations → Stage 0 EDA → Stage 1 Baseline → Stage 2 Deterministic → Stage 3 Features → Stage 4 Hybrid & submission. Each stage builds on the previous and is scored on the same holdout.

1,782 series 16-day horizon 28,512 predictions scored by RMSLE

Every number in this guide is real — reproduced from the competition CSVs. Companion docs: analyze/README.md, analyze/concepts/*, analyze/data-traps/*, analyze/eda/*. A few prediction examples are labelled illustrative (we have no trained model yet); everything else is verified.

Foundations · what we predict

What are we actually predicting?

A supermarket chain in Ecuador wants to predict daily sales. There are 54 stores, each selling 33 product families (BEVERAGES, PRODUCE, AUTOMOTIVE, …), recorded once per day.

Given years of history (2013-01-01 → 2017-08-15), predict sales for the next 16 days (2017-08-16 → 2017-08-31) for every store + family. That fixed window has a name we reuse everywhere: the horizon (= 16 days).

Key word · series

A series is the day-by-day sales history of one product family at one store — the "thing that has a past and a future." Almost every operation happens per series, never mixing across them.

54 stores × 33 families = 1,782 series

Foundations · the metric · interactive

The metric: RMSLE

Read the name backwards and it's a recipe, one word per step:

WordStep
Errorhow wrong each prediction is: prediction − actual
Logarithmicbut first take the log of both numbers
Squaredsquare each error (too-high and too-low both count)
Meanaverage all those squared errors
Roottake the square root at the end
 
RMSLE = sqrt( mean( (log1p(prediction) − log1p(actual))² ) )
Why the log?

It measures relative (ratio) error, not the raw gap. Predict 15 when truth is 5, and predict 3,000 when truth is 1,000 — both are "3× too high," equally bad. Because log(a) − log(b) = log(a/b), RMSLE asks "how many times off were you?" That's how a tiny family and a huge family count fairly.

log1p(x) = log(1+x) so log1p(0)=0 (no crash on zero sales); its inverse expm1 returns real units. Two consequences we apply to every model: we train in log space, and we clip predictions to ≥ 0.

Foundations · the scoreboard

How do we know a model is good? An honest holdout

A model always looks brilliant on the data it trained on — it has effectively seen the answers. The only honest signal is its error on data it did not train on. We keep a private slice of our own data as an instant, unlimited scoreboard: a holdout.

Time-series rule

Random K-fold cheats here. Validate on a day in the middle of history and the model trains on days that come after it — it sees the future relative to what it predicts. The split must be a clean cut in time.

train: 2013-01-01 ........... 2017-07-30  │  holdout: 2017-07-31 → 2017-08-15
       (model learns from the past)        │  (model predicts forward, blind)

We hold out the last 16 training days — same length, same "predict 16 days forward" shape as the real task.

The holdout is NOT test.csv — keep them straight

Foundations · the guard rails

The five rules that keep us honest

Everything in Foundations distills into five rules — and they're enforced in code, so a mistake fails loudly instead of silently corrupting a score.

  1. No leakage. Every feature must be knowable at prediction time. Lags use strictly past days; oil is forward-filled, never interpolated; transactions are lag-only. Guard: assert_no_leak().
  2. Gap-free before features. The Trap-1 reindex runs before any lag or seasonal feature, so "7 rows back" always means "7 days back." Guard: assert_gapfree().
  3. Lags must clear the horizon (≥ 16 days). The last day we predict (Aug 31) is 16 days past our last real data (Aug 15). We forecast all 16 days at once (direct forecasting) with a base lag of 16. See the lag-horizon deep dive.
  4. Time-respecting validation. One 16-day holdout mirroring the real horizon; train strictly on earlier data; random K-fold forbidden.
  5. Consistent metric handling. RMSLE computed on log1p, predictions clipped ≥ 0, applied identically to every model so scores are truly comparable.
Meta-lesson

When a rule matters, encode it as an assertion. A guard that raises the moment you break a rule is worth more than a comment reminding you not to.

Data integrity & the gap-free reindex

Stage 0 is the learner's first real step: look, count, and check the data is what we think it is before building anything. The 7 CSVs load cleanly; the headline counts confirm 54 stores × 33 families = 1,782 series over 1,688 days.

Stage 0 — EDA · trend

The slow drift over ~4.6 years

The first thing we notice: sales are not stationary — they're growing.

  • Mean daily total roughly doubles: ~386k in 2013 → ~856k in 2017.
  • Growth decelerates: big early jumps, smaller later ones — a curve, not a straight line.
  • Heavy-tailed families: the top ~6 families account for ~83% of all sales (GROCERY I dominates); many families are tiny.
  • Shape is shared, size is not: most families show the same upward drift + seasonal wiggle, but on wildly different scales.
Implication for modeling

A trend term is essential, and we should allow mild curvature (a straight line under-shoots one end and over-shoots the other). Model per series/family in log1p space so small families aren't swamped by the giants.

Stage 0 — EDA · seasonality

Two repeating rhythms: weekly & annual

Weekly

Weekend runs ≈ 39% above weekday average — the weekend/weekday ratio is ≈ 1.39. Sunday peaks (≈ 821,794), Thursday troughs (≈ 503,173). That's the chain-wide average; individual series differ — store 1's GROCERY I actually dips on Sunday.

Weekly shape — store 1, GROCERY I (mean sales by weekday, real)

Roughly flat Mon–Sat (~2.3–2.8k) with a sharp Sunday dip to ~1031. A weekend-peaking series would simply have a different shape — and the model adapts per series.

Annual

Clear December peak (≈ 782,483 mean per month) and February trough (≈ 571,895), with a July bump. We used mean-per-month (not sum) because training ends mid-Aug 2017, so Sep–Dec have one fewer year of data. Even missing its highest-sales year, December still tops the profile — that's how strong the holiday surge is.

Discovery: the periodogram (a "prism" for time series)

We don't have to assume the data has a weekly cycle — we can let the data show it. A periodogram splits the wiggly daily-totals curve into pure sin/cos waves of every possible period and measures how much of the variation lives at each cycle length. A tall spike at 7 days means "a strong 7-day repeat lives in here." The tool that does the splitting is the FFT — think of it as a "cycle-finder."

The FFT speaks in frequency (cycles/day); we think in period (days/cycle). period = 1 / frequency. Long, slow cycles → low frequency; short, fast cycles → high frequency.

For each candidate cycle the FFT fits a sin and a cos to the data and reads off two weights a, b. The power at that cycle is a² + b² — "how much energy lives at this period," regardless of where its peak falls. Two presentation choices keep the picture honest:

  • Subtract the mean first so the average level doesn't dominate at frequency 0.
  • Show 2–60 days on a log y-axis so the slow multi-year growth doesn't dwarf the weekly spike, and smaller spikes stay visible.
What ours shows

Tallest spike at 7 days (weekly) → a clear second spike at 3.5 days (the 2nd harmonic, present because the weekend jump is sharp, not a smooth sine) → a minor bump near 15 days. The annual cycle is outside the 2–60 band and ~4.6 years is too few repeats to resolve cleanly — that's why the annual signal is read from the month profile above, not the periodogram.

Implication for modeling

~2–3 weekly Fourier harmonics (the sharp weekend jump needs the 3.5-day 2nd harmonic) and ~3–5 annual harmonics for the smooth yearly shape. Leave the sharp December spike to explicit holiday features, not more harmonics. Mechanics in Stage 2 · Fourier.

Reference: analyze/concepts/periodogram.md, analyze/eda/03-seasonality.md.

Stage 0 — EDA · calendar effects

Holidays and paydays — not what they look like

The holiday table is NOT a yes/no flag

350 rows. 12 have transferred = True (the holiday did not happen on its listed date — a separate Transfer row carries the real date). There are Bridge days and 5 Work Day rows (a normally-off day made into a working day). locale is National / Regional / Local — so a holiday doesn't apply to every store.

National-holiday lift

82 national day-off dates in the train span. Open national holidays average ≈ 1.20× a normal day (≈ 756k vs ≈ 632k); the 4 Christmases = 0 (closed). Caveat: not weekday-controlled and smaller than the weekly swing (≈ 1.39), so treat 1.20 as suggestive, not precise.

Payday is a wave, not a single-day spike

A naive "is it the 15th or month-end?" flag gives only ≈ 1.01× — negligible. But the day-of-month profile reveals a real cycle: a start-of-month surge (day 2 ≈ 743k vs mid-month trough ≈ 599k → ≈ 1.24×) and a smaller post-15th bump.

Implication for modeling

Build an effective holiday calendar (reconcile transferred / bridge / work-day; scope by locale: National → all stores, Regional → that state, Local → that city). Encode days-since-payday or a short post-payday window — not a single-day flag.

Reference: analyze/eda/04-calendar-holidays.md, analyze/data-traps/03-holidays.md.

Stage 0 — EDA · external signals

Promotions and oil price

onpromotion — leak-free, same-day

Present in both train and test with 0 test nulls (promotions are planned ahead), so we feed it directly to the model without lagging. Started 2014-04-01 (all zeros before); 20.4% of open rows have a promotion; mean sales rises steeply across promo buckets.

Don't size the lift from EDA

The raw promo lift is doubly confounded — by family (big families promote and sell more) and by time/trend (promo rows are all 2014-04+, the higher-trend period). Leave the magnitude to the model; remember it has no signal for 2013–early 2014.

Oil — gap-fill first, then judge

1,218 trading-day rows; 43 blank prices (including the first day); 486 missing calendar days (weekends/holidays) = 529 holes on a daily calendar → 0 after forward-fill (+ a single back-fill for the leading 2013-01-01 blank). Forward-fill is past-only (leak-free); interpolation is rejected because it averages a value after the gap — that's the future.

Oil is a candidate, not a proven driver

Oil price crashed (2014 avg ≈ 93 → 2016 avg ≈ 43) while sales grew, so a raw correlation looks dramatic at −0.62. Detrended (month-over-month % changes) it collapses to ≈ 0.05 ≈ 0. The strong "relationship" was a trend artifact — same trap as crediting a routine pattern to a special event. Oil may still matter per-region/family or lagged — the holdout decides.

Reference: analyze/eda/05-promotions-oil.md, analyze/data-traps/02-oil-gaps.md, analyze/data-traps/06-transactions-past-only.md.

Stage 0 — EDA · one-off events

The 2016 earthquake — anatomy of an anomaly

A magnitude-7.8 earthquake hit on 2016-04-16. Versus an 8-week pre-quake baseline (≈ 764,910):

  • Week +0 (the impact week) jumped to ≈ 1.41× on relief buying; peak 2016-04-18 hit 1,345,921 ≈ 1.76×.
  • Week +1 was already back to ≈ 1.00×. The acute spike is ≈ 1 week, not weeks.
  • The apparent "Week +2 echo" is not the earthquake — it's the start-of-month payday surge + May 1 Labour Day. Proof: the same Apr 30 – May 6 window is actually higher in 2017 (≈ 959k, no quake) than 2016 (≈ 901k).
Lesson

Don't credit a routine pattern to a special event — the same spurious-correlation trap as oil → sales. And don't delete anomalous rows; that re-opens the gap-free calendar. Label them with an earthquake-window flag (a deliberately generous ≈ 2016-04-16 → mid-May envelope) so the model absorbs the shock as a known event. The holidays file even marks the aftermath as an Event row Terremoto Manabi+15.

Closures, while we're here

Only the 4 Dec 25 are true gaps (zero sales, restored in the reindex). The 4 Jan 1 are recorded near-zero days (stores barely open) and are already covered by calendar features — no extra flag needed.

Reference: analyze/eda/06-anomalies.md, analyze/data-traps/04-earthquake-anomaly.md.

Stage 0 — EDA · what could go wrong

The six silent traps

Stage 0's EDA also surfaces the ways real data quietly breaks a model. Ordered easiest → subtlest. Each follows the same shape: what you see → why it quietly hurts → the fix → where it lives in code. A theme runs through several — leakage: letting the model use information it would not have at prediction time.

▶1 Missing calendar days (the counting trap) ✅ Fixed

What you see: the store closes on some days — notably every Dec 25 — so those dates have no row at all. They're absent, not recorded as a zero. Series store=1, AUTOMOTIVE has 1,684 rows over a 1,688-day span → 4 missing days, all Christmases.

Why it quietly hurts: time-series features count rows. After a gap, "7 rows back" is no longer "7 days back" — your "same weekday last week" lag points at the wrong day, and seasonal sine/cosine terms slide out of phase. Nothing errors; the model just gets worse.

The fix: rebuild each series onto a complete daily calendar and fill the missing days with sales = 0 (the store sold nothing — which is true, and keeps the row spacing regular). Tag each inserted row was_closed = True so nothing is lost.

  3,000,888  (gaps where the store was shut)
+     7,128  (4 Christmases × 1,782 series, restored as sales = 0)
─────────
  3,008,016  (a complete, gap-free grid: 1,782 series × 1,688 days)

Where: src/data.py :: reindex_series_gapfree(), checked by src/validation.py :: assert_gapfree().

▶2 Oil price gaps + blanks (your first taste of leakage) ⏳ Planned

What you see: Ecuador's economy is oil-dependent, so oil price is a plausible driver. But oil.csv has two holes: oil only trades on business days (weekends/holidays have no row), and 43 rows exist with an empty price.

The fix: build a continuous daily calendar, left-join oil, then forward-fill — carry the last known price forward (one leading back-fill for the very first blank day).

Leakage warning

Why not interpolation? It averages the value before and after the gap — and "after" is the future. Using it means the model peeks at information it wouldn't have. Forward-fill only looks backward, so it's safe.

Where: oil features in src/features.py.

▶3 Holidays aren't a simple yes/no (domain modeling) ⏳ Planned

What you see: holidays_events.csv looks like a "was it a holiday?" table, but isn't. A row with transferred = True means the holiday did not happen on its listed date — a separate Transfer row carries the real date. There are also Bridge days and Work Day rows, and locale (National/Regional/Local) means a holiday doesn't apply to every store.

The fix: compute an effective holiday calendar — drop the transferred originals, honor the Transfer/Bridge/Work Day rows, and scope by locale: National → all stores; Regional → that state; Local → that city.

Lesson

A column named like a feature isn't automatically a usable feature. Understanding the domain is part of feature engineering.

Where: holiday features in src/features.py.

▶4 The 2016 earthquake anomaly ⏳ Planned

What you see: a magnitude-7.8 earthquake on 2016-04-16 caused a relief-driven sales spike of ≈ 1.41× the pre-quake baseline (peak ≈ 1.76× on Apr 18).

Why it quietly hurts: a one-off spike isn't a repeating pattern. Left unmarked, your trend and seasonality try to "explain" it and get bent out of shape.

The fix: add a binary (or decaying) earthquake-window flag (~2016-04-16 → mid-May) so the model absorbs the spike as a known event. We do not delete those rows — that would re-open a hole in the gap-free index from Trap 1.

Lesson

Don't delete anomalies — label them.

Where: calendar features in src/features.py.

▶5 New / sparse series (the coverage trap) ⏳ Planned

What you see: some store + family pairs have little or no meaningful history (a barely-selling family, or one that only started recently).

Why it quietly hurts: a complex model can't learn a pattern that isn't there — but the submission must contain a value for all 28,512 rows. A missing/NaN prediction is invalid.

The fix: for thin series, fall back to a simple seasonal-naive / near-zero prediction. See Stage 1 baselines for exactly how these work.

Where: the fallback path in src/models.py.

▶6 Transactions are past-only (a hard constraint) ⚠️ Constraint

What you see: transactions.csv is rich and tempting — but it is not provided for the 16-day test horizon. You don't know a store's transaction count on a day you're predicting.

Leakage warning

Use same-day transactions and validation looks fantastic — because in validation you happen to have that column. On the real future it doesn't exist, so the model collapses.

The fix: use transactions only as a lagged feature (e.g. transactions 7+ days ago, which are known by prediction time).

Where: lag features in src/features.py.

Stage 1 · the floor any model must beat

Forecast baselines: seasonal-naive & near-zero

The simplest possible ways to predict — no training, no maths. Stage 1's job is to score these on the holdout and record the number to beat. They also serve as the fallback rules for series too thin to model (Trap 5). They predict the future 16 days; they do not change any past data.

how it works Seasonal-naive = copy last week forward

Naive = don't learn anything; copy the past forward. Seasonal = copy from the same point in the previous cycle (here, the weekly cycle).

predict next Monday  = last Monday's sales
predict next Tuesday = last Tuesday's sales
…repeat last week's 7-day pattern across the 16-day horizon

Sample data — store 1, GROCERY I (real)

The last known week (7 days ending Tue Aug 15, 2017):

Last weekWed 8/9Thu 8/10Fri 8/11Sat 8/12Sun 8/13Mon 8/14Tue 8/15
sales271925911270163095224072508

Seasonal-naive copies each weekday forward — the 7-day pattern just repeats across the horizon, preserving this series' own shape (here a Sunday dip to 952 against midweek highs of ~2.5–2.7k).

how it works Near-zero = "sells nothing → predict nothing"

If a series has essentially no sales ever, the safest forecast is ≈ 0. Example — store 1, BOOKS, whose last week is all zeros:

Last 7 days (store 1, BOOKS)0000000

→ predict 0 for all 16 horizon days. Nothing to copy.

important Who decides which rule? (not the model)

A common misconception: the trained model does not choose between the main prediction, seasonal-naive, and near-zero. The choice is made before modeling by a simple rule-based gate (an if check) on each series' history. Thin series are peeled off and never reach the model.

For each of the 1,782 series:
   ├─ Has enough history?  ──►  use the MAIN model (the learned one)
   └─ Too thin?            ──►  use a FALLBACK rule
                                   ├─ a little history to copy  ►  seasonal-naive
                                   └─ basically no sales ever   ►  near-zero (~0)

A trained model is a function: it always returns a number — it can't output a blank. The risk for a no-history series isn't emptiness; it's that the number is unreliable garbage. So we route thin series around the model (the cleaner option), train only on healthy ones, then stitch both prediction sets back together into all 28,512 rows.

Where: the fallback path in src/models.py; scored by rmsle() on the holdout, exactly like every later stage.

Stage 2 · trend + seasonality (date-only features)

The deterministic backbone

Stage 2 fits a model whose features depend only on the date: a trend (slow drift, Stage 0 confirmed) plus Fourier seasonal terms (weekly + annual, justified by the periodogram and the month profile). "Deterministic" means computable for any future date — perfect for forecasting, because we always know the date.

how it works Why sin + cos represent a cycle

A cycle is a wave; the natural way to write one is sin and cos. For a weekly pattern (period = 7): sin(2π·day/7) and cos(2π·day/7) each complete one full wave every 7 days. Why both? A single sine has a fixed peak position; combining a sin and a cos (with weights the model learns) lets it place the peak anywhere, at any height — so with just 2 numbers per cycle a plain linear regression fits "this series peaks Saturday, that one Sunday."

Sample data — exact sin/cos vs real weekday means (store 1, GROCERY I)

The model learns two weights (w_sin, w_cos) plus an intercept so intercept + w_sin·sin + w_cos·cos traces this shape. Two columns reconstruct the weekly rhythm and extend cleanly into any future date. (A sharp single-day dip like Sunday's needs a couple more Fourier pairs — the higher harmonics — to pin down exactly. That's why the periodogram's 3.5-day spike matters.)

tooling CalendarFourier & DeterministicProcess

CalendarFourier generates those sin/cos columns from the dates. Instead of 365 separate "is it Jan 1 / Jan 2 / …" columns for the yearly cycle, you ask for a few Fourier pairs and get a handful of smooth columns approximating the whole annual wave. Stage 0 told us how many: ~2–3 weekly and ~3–5 annual harmonics.

DeterministicProcess assembles the full time-feature table: a constant, a trend (1, 2, 3, … counting upward), and the seasonal Fourier terms. We allow mild curvature for the trend because Stage 0 showed growth is decelerating.

why gaps wreck it "Out of phase" — in numbers

Phase = where you are in the cycle. The sin/cos value comes from the row's position, assuming consecutive days. Correct (no gap):

Now drop the Sat Aug 12 row (a closed day). Everything after shifts up one:

Sunday's 952 sales are now paired with Saturday's sin value. Every weekday past the gap is mislabelled by one — silent corruption. This is exactly why Trap 1's reindex must run before any seasonal feature.

Punchline

Fill the calendar first (so position = real date again), then build Fourier seasonality.

Stage 2 · the lag floor

The 16-day lag horizon (why lags must be ≥ 16)

The deterministic backbone uses only date features. Once we add lag features in Stage 3 ("sales N days ago"), they're built from past sales. But our prediction window is 16 days long and we have no real sales inside it. That puts a hard floor on how short a lag we can use.

The rule

The last day you predict is 16 days past your last real data. A lag that must work for the whole horizon has to reach back at least 16 days. In general: minimum usable lag = horizon length (H).

The design decision: direct forecasting with base lag = 16

ApproachHowTrade-off
Direct (chosen)Predict all 16 days at once, using only real lags ≥ 16 (lag_16, lag_17, … ; rolling windows anchored at shift(16))One feature matrix, no error compounding
Recursive (not chosen)Predict Aug 16, treat it as real, use as lag_1 for Aug 17, …Allows short lags, but errors snowball across 16 days
Direct per stepA separate model per horizon day (day-1 uses lag_1, day-16 uses lag_16)Freshest legal lag per day, but 16 models
Long horizons → lean on seasonality

A 1-year horizon would need lag_365 as its shortest lag — nearly useless. The longer the horizon, the less lags help and the more you lean on date-only features (trend + Fourier seasonality, holidays), computable for any future date. That's why the modelling is staged: a deterministic backbone first (Stage 2), lags added on top as a short-horizon booster (Stage 3) — never a replacement.

Where: lag/rolling features in src/features.py; enforced by the base-lag ≥ 16 guard in src/validation.py :: assert_no_leak().

Stage 3 · learned features on top of the backbone

Lags, holidays, promotions, oil, earthquake

Stage 3 adds the features that depend on past sales or external context — everything Stage 2's date-only backbone cannot see. Every feature has to clear the no leakage and lag ≥ 16 rules.

Lag & rolling features (the short-horizon booster)

Per series: lag_16, lag_17, …, and rolling windows anchored at shift(16) (e.g. 7-day rolling mean of sales as of 16 days ago). Same shape, different statistic — they tell the model "what level was this series at, 16+ days ago." For transactions (Trap 6), lag-only — never same-day.

Holiday features (effective calendar, not the raw file)

Build the effective calendar from holidays_events.csv: drop the 12 transferred originals, honor Transfer / Bridge / Work Day rows, scope by locale (National → all stores, Regional → that state, Local → that city). Output one or more clean flags per row. Don't shove the raw rows in.

Payday features (a wave, not a flag)

From Stage 0: the naive "is it the 15th?" flag is ≈ 1.01× (negligible), but a real ≈ 1.24× cycle exists across the month. Encode days-since-payday or a short post-payday window — that lets the model express the wave smoothly.

Promotion feature (the easy one)

onpromotion is delivered same-day in the test horizon (no nulls), so we feed it directly to the model — no lag. It has no signal before 2014-04-01, so don't design features that assume it has always existed.

Oil feature (forward-filled, modest expectations)

Forward-fill first (no interpolation — that peeks at the future). Use as a feature, but remember Stage 0's bonus result: the raw oil↔sales correlation of −0.62 collapses to ≈ 0.05 after detrending. Don't expect a miracle; the holdout decides.

Earthquake-window flag

A deliberately generous binary (or decaying) flag spanning ≈ 2016-04-16 → mid-May 2016. From Stage 0: the acute spike (≈ 1.41×, peak 1.76× on Apr 18) is only ~1 week — but a generous window is fine; the model can downweight the tail.

Stage 3 in one sentence

Every new column is knowable at prediction time: lags reach back ≥ 16 days; oil is forward-filled; transactions are lag-only; promo is the leak-free same-day signal. assert_no_leak() raises the moment you forget.

Where: src/features.py (every feature builder composes through here).

Stage 4 · combine, decide granularity, submit

Hybrid model & the actual submission

The hybrid idea

Stage 2's deterministic backbone captures the slow drift and the calendar rhythm cleanly. Stage 3's feature model captures the short-horizon corrections — recent level, promotions, the earthquake window. A hybrid fits Stage 2 first, takes its residuals (what's left unexplained), and lets Stage 3 predict those. Two specialists, each on the problem it's good at.

Granularity decision

One model for all 1,782 series? One per family? One per store-family? Stage 4 picks the level using the holdout RMSLE — finer granularity captures local patterns but starves on data; coarser pools more data but smears patterns. The same five rules apply to every choice.

Fallback stitching (Stage 1 returns)

Thin series were peeled off before training and predicted by the Stage 1 fallback (seasonal-naive / near-zero). Stitch their predictions back in so the final output covers all 28,512 rows.

Final fit on everything, then predict

Once the best approach is chosen on the holdout, re-fit the chosen model on the full training data (including the 16 holdout days — the freshest trend and seasonality live there) before predicting the real test.csv.
