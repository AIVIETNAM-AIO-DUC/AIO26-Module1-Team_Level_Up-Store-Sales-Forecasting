Stage 0 · look before you model

EDA — the evidence behind every later decision

Stage 0 is the learner's first real step: look, count, and check the data is what we think it is before building anything. Every modeling choice in Stages 1–4 traces back to a finding on this page.

Source: analyze/eda/* and analyze/data-traps/*. Every number here has a ## Verify snippet on its source page.

Stage 0 — EDA · overview

Data integrity & the gap-free reindex

The 7 CSVs load cleanly; the headline counts confirm 54 stores × 33 families = 1,782 series over 1,688 days. The first repair — Stage 0's only structural change — is rebuilding every series onto a complete daily calendar (see Trap 1). Read the arithmetic:

original rows   : 3,000,888    ← what Kaggle gave us
reindexed rows  : 3,008,016    ← after filling gaps
expected        : 1,782 × 1,688 = 3,008,016
rows inserted   :     7,128    ← 4 per series (the four Christmases)

Total = product, and 7,128 / 1,782 = 4 — clean evidence the reindex did exactly the right thing. Where: src/data.py :: reindex_series_gapfree(); guarded by assert_gapfree().

Stage 0 — EDA · trend

The slow drift over ~4.6 years

The first thing we notice: sales are not stationary — they're growing.

  • Mean daily total roughly doubles: ~386k in 2013 → ~856k in 2017.
  • Growth decelerates: big early jumps, smaller later ones — a curve, not a straight line.
  • Heavy-tailed families: the top ~6 families account for ~83% of all sales (GROCERY I dominates); many families are tiny.
  • Shape is shared, size is not: most families show the same upward drift + seasonal wiggle, but on wildly different scales.
Implication for modeling

A trend term is essential, and we should allow mild curvature (a straight line under-shoots one end and over-shoots the other). Model per series/family in log1p space so small families aren't swamped by the giants. Mechanics in Stage 2.

Stage 0 — EDA · seasonality

Two repeating rhythms: weekly & annual

Weekly

Weekend runs ≈ 39% above weekday average — the weekend/weekday ratio is ≈ 1.39. Sunday peaks (≈ 821,794), Thursday troughs (≈ 503,173). That's the chain-wide average; individual series differ — store 1's GROCERY I actually dips on Sunday.

Weekly shape — store 1, GROCERY I (mean sales by weekday, real)

Roughly flat Mon–Sat (~2.3–2.8k) with a sharp Sunday dip to ~1031. A weekend-peaking series would simply have a different shape — and the model adapts per series.

Annual

Clear December peak (≈ 782,483 mean per month) and February trough (≈ 571,895), with a July bump. We used mean-per-month (not sum) because training ends mid-Aug 2017, so Sep–Dec have one fewer year of data. Even missing its highest-sales year, December still tops the profile — that's how strong the holiday surge is.

Discovery: the periodogram (a "prism" for time series)

We don't have to assume the data has a weekly cycle — we can let the data show it. A periodogram splits the wiggly daily-totals curve into pure sin/cos waves of every possible period and measures how much of the variation lives at each cycle length. A tall spike at 7 days means "a strong 7-day repeat lives in here." The tool that does the splitting is the FFT — think of it as a "cycle-finder."

The FFT speaks in frequency (cycles/day); we think in period (days/cycle). period = 1 / frequency. Long, slow cycles → low frequency; short, fast cycles → high frequency.

For each candidate cycle the FFT fits a sin and a cos to the data and reads off two weights a, b. The power at that cycle is a² + b² — "how much energy lives at this period," regardless of where its peak falls. Two presentation choices keep the picture honest:

  • Subtract the mean first so the average level doesn't dominate at frequency 0.
  • Show 2–60 days on a log y-axis so the slow multi-year growth doesn't dwarf the weekly spike, and smaller spikes stay visible.
What ours shows

Tallest spike at 7 days (weekly) → a clear second spike at 3.5 days (the 2nd harmonic, present because the weekend jump is sharp, not a smooth sine) → a minor bump near 15 days. The annual cycle is outside the 2–60 band and ~4.6 years is too few repeats to resolve cleanly — that's why the annual signal is read from the month profile above, not the periodogram.

Implication for modeling

~2–3 weekly Fourier harmonics (the sharp weekend jump needs the 3.5-day 2nd harmonic) and ~3–5 annual harmonics for the smooth yearly shape. Leave the sharp December spike to explicit holiday features, not more harmonics. Mechanics in Stage 2 · Fourier.

Reference: analyze/concepts/periodogram.md, analyze/eda/03-seasonality.md.

Stage 0 — EDA · calendar effects

Holidays and paydays — not what they look like

The holiday table is NOT a yes/no flag

350 rows. 12 have transferred = True (the holiday did not happen on its listed date — a separate Transfer row carries the real date). There are Bridge days and 5 Work Day rows (a normally-off day made into a working day). locale is National / Regional / Local — so a holiday doesn't apply to every store.

National-holiday lift

82 national day-off dates in the train span. Open national holidays average ≈ 1.20× a normal day (≈ 756k vs ≈ 632k); the 4 Christmases = 0 (closed). Caveat: not weekday-controlled and smaller than the weekly swing (≈ 1.39), so treat 1.20 as suggestive, not precise.

Payday is a wave, not a single-day spike

A naive "is it the 15th or month-end?" flag gives only ≈ 1.01× — negligible. But the day-of-month profile reveals a real cycle: a start-of-month surge (day 2 ≈ 743k vs mid-month trough ≈ 599k → ≈ 1.24×) and a smaller post-15th bump.

Implication for modeling

Build an effective holiday calendar (reconcile transferred / bridge / work-day; scope by locale: National → all stores, Regional → that state, Local → that city). Encode days-since-payday or a short post-payday window — not a single-day flag. Implemented in Stage 3.

Reference: analyze/eda/04-calendar-holidays.md, analyze/data-traps/03-holidays.md.

Stage 0 — EDA · external signals

Promotions and oil price

onpromotion — leak-free, same-day

Present in both train and test with 0 test nulls (promotions are planned ahead), so we feed it directly to the model without lagging. Started 2014-04-01 (all zeros before); 20.4% of open rows have a promotion; mean sales rises steeply across promo buckets.

Don't size the lift from EDA

The raw promo lift is doubly confounded — by family (big families promote and sell more) and by time/trend (promo rows are all 2014-04+, the higher-trend period). Leave the magnitude to the model; remember it has no signal for 2013–early 2014.

Oil — gap-fill first, then judge

1,218 trading-day rows; 43 blank prices (including the first day); 486 missing calendar days (weekends/holidays) = 529 holes on a daily calendar → 0 after forward-fill (+ a single back-fill for the leading 2013-01-01 blank). Forward-fill is past-only (leak-free); interpolation is rejected because it averages a value after the gap — that's the future.

Oil is a candidate, not a proven driver

Oil price crashed (2014 avg ≈ 93 → 2016 avg ≈ 43) while sales grew, so a raw correlation looks dramatic at −0.62. Detrended (month-over-month % changes) it collapses to ≈ 0.05 ≈ 0. The strong "relationship" was a trend artifact — same trap as crediting a routine pattern to a special event. Oil may still matter per-region/family or lagged — the holdout decides.

Reference: analyze/eda/05-promotions-oil.md, analyze/data-traps/02-oil-gaps.md, analyze/data-traps/06-transactions-past-only.md.

Stage 0 — EDA · one-off events

The 2016 earthquake — anatomy of an anomaly

A magnitude-7.8 earthquake hit on 2016-04-16. Versus an 8-week pre-quake baseline (≈ 764,910):

  • Week +0 (the impact week) jumped to ≈ 1.41× on relief buying; peak 2016-04-18 hit 1,345,921 ≈ 1.76×.
  • Week +1 was already back to ≈ 1.00×. The acute spike is ≈ 1 week, not weeks.
  • The apparent "Week +2 echo" is not the earthquake — it's the start-of-month payday surge + May 1 Labour Day. Proof: the same Apr 30 – May 6 window is actually higher in 2017 (≈ 959k, no quake) than 2016 (≈ 901k).
Lesson

Don't credit a routine pattern to a special event — the same spurious-correlation trap as oil → sales. And don't delete anomalous rows; that re-opens the gap-free calendar. Label them with an earthquake-window flag (a deliberately generous ≈ 2016-04-16 → mid-May envelope) so the model absorbs the shock as a known event. The holidays file even marks the aftermath as an Event row Terremoto Manabi+15.

Closures, while we're here

Only the 4 Dec 25 are true gaps (zero sales, restored in the reindex). The 4 Jan 1 are recorded near-zero days (stores barely open) and are already covered by calendar features — no extra flag needed.

Reference: analyze/eda/06-anomalies.md, analyze/data-traps/04-earthquake-anomaly.md.

Stage 0 — EDA · what could go wrong

The six silent traps

Ordered easiest → subtlest. Each follows the same shape: what you see → why it quietly hurts → the fix → where it lives in code. A theme runs through several — leakage: letting the model use information it would not have at prediction time.

▶1 Missing calendar days (the counting trap) ✅ Fixed

What you see: the store closes on some days — notably every Dec 25 — so those dates have no row at all. They're absent, not recorded as a zero. Series store=1, AUTOMOTIVE has 1,684 rows over a 1,688-day span → 4 missing days, all Christmases.

Why it quietly hurts: time-series features locate "the past" by row position in the DataFrame, not by calendar date. df["sales"].shift(7) simply means "take the value of the row 7 positions back" — it never looks at the date column. The two only agree when no day is missing.

Concrete example, series store=1, AUTOMOTIVE around Christmas 2016:

indexdatesales
100Dec 205
101Dec 216
102Dec 224
103Dec 237
104Dec 248
—Dec 25 (no row — store closed)—
105Dec 265
106Dec 276
107Dec 289

At row index=107 (Dec 28), shift(7) grabs index=100 → Dec 20. But "7 days before Dec 28" should be Dec 21. Off by one day. The lag that was supposed to be "last Wednesday" is now "last Tuesday," and seasonal sine/cosine terms drift by the same amount. Nothing errors; the model just gets worse.

The fix: rebuild each series onto a complete daily calendar and fill the missing days with sales = 0 (the store sold nothing — which is true, and keeps the row spacing regular). Tag each inserted row was_closed = True so nothing is lost.

  3,000,888  (gaps where the store was shut)
+     7,128  (4 Christmases × 1,782 series, restored as sales = 0)
─────────
  3,008,016  (a complete, gap-free grid: 1,782 series × 1,688 days)

Where: src/data.py :: reindex_series_gapfree(), checked by src/validation.py :: assert_gapfree().

▶2 Oil price gaps + blanks (your first taste of leakage) ⏳ Planned

What you see: Ecuador's economy is oil-dependent, so oil price is a plausible driver. But oil.csv has two holes: oil only trades on business days (weekends/holidays have no row), and 43 rows exist with an empty price.

The fix: build a continuous daily calendar, left-join oil, then forward-fill — carry the last known price forward (one leading back-fill for the very first blank day).

Leakage warning

Why not interpolation? It averages the value before and after the gap — and "after" is the future. Using it means the model peeks at information it wouldn't have. Forward-fill only looks backward, so it's safe.

Where: oil features in src/features.py.

▶3 Holidays aren't a simple yes/no (domain modeling) ⏳ Planned

What you see: holidays_events.csv looks like a "was it a holiday?" table, but isn't. A row with transferred = True means the holiday did not happen on its listed date — a separate Transfer row carries the real date. There are also Bridge days and Work Day rows, and locale (National/Regional/Local) means a holiday doesn't apply to every store.

The fix: compute an effective holiday calendar — drop the transferred originals, honor the Transfer/Bridge/Work Day rows, and scope by locale: National → all stores; Regional → that state; Local → that city.

Lesson

A column named like a feature isn't automatically a usable feature. Understanding the domain is part of feature engineering.

Where: holiday features in src/features.py.

▶4 The 2016 earthquake anomaly ⏳ Planned

What you see: a magnitude-7.8 earthquake on 2016-04-16 caused a relief-driven sales spike of ≈ 1.41× the pre-quake baseline (peak ≈ 1.76× on Apr 18).

Why it quietly hurts: a one-off spike isn't a repeating pattern. Left unmarked, your trend and seasonality try to "explain" it and get bent out of shape.

The fix: add a binary (or decaying) earthquake-window flag (~2016-04-16 → mid-May) so the model absorbs the spike as a known event. We do not delete those rows — that would re-open a hole in the gap-free index from Trap 1.

Lesson

Don't delete anomalies — label them.

Where: calendar features in src/features.py.

▶5 New / sparse series (the coverage trap) ⏳ Planned

What you see: some store + family pairs have little or no meaningful history (a barely-selling family, or one that only started recently).

Why it quietly hurts: a complex model can't learn a pattern that isn't there — but the submission must contain a value for all 28,512 rows. A missing/NaN prediction is invalid.

The fix: for thin series, fall back to a simple seasonal-naive / near-zero prediction. See Stage 1 baselines for the mechanics.

Where: the fallback path in src/models.py.

▶6 Transactions are past-only (a hard constraint) ⚠️ Constraint

What you see: transactions.csv is rich and tempting — but it is not provided for the 16-day test horizon. You don't know a store's transaction count on a day you're predicting.

Leakage warning

Use same-day transactions and validation looks fantastic — because in validation you happen to have that column. On the real future it doesn't exist, so the model collapses.

The fix: use transactions only as a lagged feature (e.g. transactions 7+ days ago, which are known by prediction time).

Where: lag features in src/features.py.
